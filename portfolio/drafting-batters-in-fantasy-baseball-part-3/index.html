Drafting Batters in Fantasy Baseball, part 3

There is another way!

In doing research for writing this blog post, I found this article on setting up my own rankings based on my league scoring categories, using z-scores. It’s a simple statistic that identifies who is above the mean, and by how much. This is so simple, I was kicking myself for not doing it when I did my draft. Here’s how it works.

Create Z-Scores

bat_z <- batters %>%
  filter(PA >= 300) %>%
  select(playerid, position, Name, Team, R, HR, RBI, SO, SB, OPS, WAR) %>%
  mutate(R_z = z_score(R),
         HR_z = z_score(HR),
         RBI_z = z_score(RBI),
         SO_z = -z_score(SO),
         SB_z = z_score(SB),
         OPS_z = z_score(OPS),
         tot_z = round((R_z + HR_z + RBI_z + SO_z + SB_z + OPS_z), 3))

I took the same batters dataset and filtered it just to include those with 300 or more plate appearances. Since I’m standardizing, I wanted to exclude players without sufficient playing time, who may have really low counts of runs, home runs, RBIs, strikeouts, or stolen bases just because of small samples. These players may also have OPS statistics in the extremes (really high, or really low) because of small samples. I didn’t want to draft players who weren’t going to play for most of the season, and 300 plate appearances is roughly two appearances per game. This effectively halved the batters in my dataset.

Once I filtered based on that criteria, I calculated the mean and standard deviation for the remaining players, and used that to calculate a z-score, indicating how extreme (either positive or negative) that player’s numbers are relative to the mean. A z-score of 4 would mean that person is four standard deviations higher than the mean (which is great if you’re looking at runs and home runs, and terrible if you’re looking at strikeouts). I calculated z-scores for all of the scoring categories, multipled strikeouts by -1 so they were all on the same scale, and then I summed them all to get an overall z-score, that tells me who is above average among all relevant scoring categories.

End of Season Results

Team Expectations vs. Reality

Up until this point, we’ve been looking at stat projections for 2018, but now that the season is over, we can see just how these projections played out. Let’s bring in the final 2018 stats from Fangraphs, and compare them to the projected stats. I’ve renamed all final stats to include the f_ prefix. While I’m at it, I also calculated z-scores for each stat, and over the total. Finally, I merged this dataset to the full batters dataset, and also to the subset of players I drafted.

end_bat_z <- read.csv("../data/post1/batters_final.csv") %>%
  rename(f_R = R,
         f_HR = HR,
         f_RBI = RBI,
         f_SO = SO,
         f_SB = SB,
         f_OPS = OPS,
         f_WAR = WAR) %>%
  filter(PA >= 300) %>%
  mutate(f_R_z = z_score(f_R),
         f_HR_z = z_score(f_HR),
         f_RBI_z = z_score(f_RBI),
         f_SO_z = -z_score(f_SO),
         f_SB_z = z_score(f_SB),
         f_OPS_z = z_score(f_OPS),
         f_tot_z = round((f_R_z + f_HR_z + f_RBI_z + f_SO_z + f_SB_z + f_OPS_z), 3),
         playerid = as.character(playerid)) %>%
  select(-Team)
  
all_final <- inner_join(end_bat_z, bat_z, by = c("playerid", "Name"))
drafted_final <- inner_join(end_bat_z, drafted, by = c("Name"))

Lessons Learned

Now that I’ve done the post-mortem, here are the top three takeaways from my exploration: 1) Don’t rely solely on WAR to draft players. 2) Use data, not personal bias (namely, stop over-drafting Giants players) 3) Pay attention to positional talent – draft outfielders earlier and catchers later.

I also did a little bit of exploration in my own league, and I found that most people don’t draft catchers in the first 10 rounds, because catchers don’t seem to make much of a difference.

Yeah, this was news to me!
